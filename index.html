Encryption is key


If your data is hacked, the files that you encrypt will be by far the safest. Reversing encrypted code is difficult for even a good hacker to do, so rather than removing yourself from the grid completely, find an encryption service for your most important files.

Protect your passwords


Your passwords should be long and complex, using every requirement allowed. That's easy to do when you have one, but ideally each account is different. You can write these down or keep them in an encrypted document, but that gets tricky if you’re on the move. Password managers help you generate and store passwords in an encrypted database. There are free options, but make sure you consider your needs. Aside from lacking in some features, free password managers tend to limit the number of stored passcodes, or the number of devices you can use it on. LastPass provides more than any other free service.

Keep your records closer with a VPN


A virtual private network (VPN) is an encryption tool for internet traffic, that takes data directly from your device to a server, while avoiding unwanted eyes. When you sign up for a VPN service, the provider owns that server, so you’ll want to pick one that’s well-known – for the right reasons, of course. Again, paid services often provide a free trial, while others have tried being entirely free via ad-support or limited bandwidth. Signal is a free encrypted app, but it can only be used for messaging.

Looking Forward


There are a lot of lines that can be drawn to give future generations less to think about, but the first step is to make privacy more affordable. Many of those lines are dependent on government decisions that we can help push for. At a lower level, companies can start considering the possibility of being hacked when making market-driving decisions. On any even lower level, however, it's up to us to make decisions that will help keep our fellow consumers' information secure. There are free resources that aren't profiting from selling data to ads, as well as nonprofit organizations whose sole mission is to keep our data, ours. There's a list of links to these organizations below – You can choose to donate, or just promote the cause. In the meantime, here are a few free tips on ways you can secure your family's digital footprint:


  • A fingerprint is more secure then a passcode, but if you must use a combination to get into your phone, use as many digits as your device allows. A four-digit passcode has 10,000 combination possibilities, but a six-digit passcode has 1,000,0000.
  • Check your devices’ internet networks frequently to clean out the ones that you don’t use on a daily basis. You never know which one you might have logged into in a moment of haste that has been compromised since or was already unsecure.
  • “Another measure that a lot of consumers should take but may not necessarily be taking yet is 2-step-protection for important log ins,” said Zuckerman. Two factor authentification is required by some services, and optional on others. When it's optional, use it.
  • The “S” in HTTPS stands for secure, so if you’re on a website that isn’t using it, use caution.
