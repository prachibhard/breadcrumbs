A Penny for Your Thoughts


Companies across industries are willing to pay large sums of money for information about what, where, when, and how people from various demographics and geographical regions make the decisions that they make. In an ideal world, this demand for consumer information would put consumers at a huge advantage.

Instead, a lack of consumer rights in the U.S. has given corporations an opportunity to obtain user data with minimal restrictions and repercussions.

While the U.S. Privacy Act of 1974 controls how government agencies collect personally identifiable information (PII), and a number of states have enacted privacy legislation, no federal legislation currently exists to address how the private sector can collect consumer data. The Federal Trade Commission Act is the closest the U.S. has come, but it wasn’t designed specifically to address the private sector. It has been used to enforce companies’ privacy policies, but doesn’t have language that requires companies to put those privacy policies in place.

When national laws fall short, as they do for data collection, consumer-facing companies like Facebook, Amazon, and Google look to state laws or industry “best practices” to govern.

In her study about “the privacy paradox” – a term referring to the disconnect between an individual’s privacy standards and behavior – Susan Athey looks at the two concepts involved in forming any privacy-related policy: Notice and Choice. The former involves telling the consumer what data will be used before it’s taken, and the latter gives the user the option to accept those terms.

While consumers are fearful of breaches, hacks, or anything else that involves data being taken from them without consent, they all too easily sign personal data away to companies for immediate gratification. Just ask the 1.37 billion daily active users on Facebook. Once a user clicks on that green button (click to see a full list of the data shared), Facebook has complied with both notice and consent.

Encryption is key


If your data is hacked, the files that you encrypt will be by far the safest. Reversing encrypted code is difficult for even a good hacker to do, so rather than removing yourself from the grid completely, find an encryption service for your most important files.

Protect your passwords


Your passwords should be long and complex, using every requirement allowed. That's easy to do when you have one, but ideally each account is different. You can write these down or keep them in an encrypted document, but that gets tricky if you’re on the move. Password managers help you generate and store passwords in an encrypted database. There are free options, but make sure you consider your needs. Aside from lacking in some features, free password managers tend to limit the number of stored passcodes, or the number of devices you can use it on. LastPass provides more than any other free service.

Keep your records closer with a VPN


A virtual private network (VPN) is an encryption tool for internet traffic, that takes data directly from your device to a server, while avoiding unwanted eyes. When you sign up for a VPN service, the provider owns that server, so you’ll want to pick one that’s well-known – for the right reasons, of course. Again, paid services often provide a free trial, while others have tried being entirely free via ad-support or limited bandwidth. Signal is a free encrypted app, but it can only be used for messaging.

Looking Forward


There are a lot of regulatory lines that can be drawn to give future generations less to think about, but a good first step is to make privacy more affordable.


At the highest level, we can push for federal legislation that can patch up any holes left by state laws or industry-driven guidelines.


Looking at the mid-level, companies can start considering the possibility of being hacked when making market-driving decisions.


But on the level closest to home, it's up to us to make decisions that will help keep our fellow consumers' information secure. There are free resources that aren't profiting from selling data to ads, as well as nonprofit organizations whose sole mission is to keep our data, ours. The most important thing to remember is that it won't work if it's a small population's effort. Encourage friends and family to use the applications and tools that safeguard your privacy for maximum impact.


At the bottom, you'll find a list of links to products and organizations to get you started. You can choose to donate money, or just promote the cause.


But first, here are a few free tips on ways you can secure your family's digital footprint:


  • A fingerprint is more secure then a passcode, but if you must use a combination to get into your phone, use as many digits as your device allows. A four-digit passcode has 10,000 combination possibilities, but a six-digit passcode has 1,000,0000.
  • Check your devices’ internet networks frequently to clean out the ones that you don’t use on a daily basis. You never know which one you might have logged into in a moment of haste that has been compromised since or was already unsecure.
  • “Another measure that a lot of consumers should take but may not necessarily be taking yet is 2-step-protection for important log ins,” said Zuckerman. Two factor authentification is required by some services, and optional on others. When it's optional, use it.
  • The “S” in HTTPS stands for secure, so if you’re on a website that isn’t using it, use caution.
